The Clean Water Crisis

The Problem

Currently, 783,000,000 people, or 11% of the world, lacks access to clean, safe water. Every year, an estimated 842,000 people die from inadequate drinking water; this translates to approximately two thousand three hundred deaths from inadequate water every day. But, the ill effects of unclean water stretch far beyond the loss of human life; the United Nations estimates that 40,000,000,000 man hours are lost every year collecting water, the equivalent of a year’s worth of labor by the entirety of France’s workforce. Additionally, it is estimated that over half of the world’s hospital beds are filled with those suffering from some type of water related illness and, in developing countries, well over 80% of illnesses are linked with unclean water and poor sanitation.

These effects in turn have a severe economic impact. The World Health Organization estimates that $24,000,000,000 is lost every year due to time spent gathering water, over $18,000,000,000 in economic benefits are lost due to the deaths from unclean water, and over $32,000,000,000 in economic benefits are lost due to decreased productivity and illnesses related to unclean water. All told, it is estimated that sub-Saharan Africa loses over four percent of its GDP each year due to inadequate access to clean water.

The Solution

New water purification technology alone will not solve the water crisis. These technologies need to be implemented with sustainability in mind to allow for clean water not for just today, but forever. Digging a new well is only a fraction of the work required to ensure continued access to safe water. All villagers need to be taught proper hygiene and sanitation in order to prevent wells from becoming contaminated by waste, thereby ensuring that the well water will continue to be clean long after construction ends and the aid workers leave. Additionally, it is important that any filtration technology used is itself sustainable. This is why the sand and gravel filtration system is the best system for most areas that currently lack access to clean water. The materials cost of a sand and gravel system is very low and, while the filtration material occasionally needs to be replaced, the filtration material can be locally sourced and therefore incur no cost to the village.

However, it is important to recognize that there is no one-size-fits-all solution to provide clean, sustainable water. This is why it is important to work with the village being served in order to understand their needs and together select a well that meets their needs.

Additionally, organizations such as Water.org are taking sustainability to a new level. Water.org offers a program called “WaterCredit” which offers mircoloans to individual families or communities which are then slowly repaid over time. These loans, once repaid, can then be redeployed elsewhere, reducing the need for subsidies and allowing for donations to be stretched further.

How to Help

You can help provide millions of people clean, accessible water through a donation to Water.org. Your money goes towards the creation of new wells in areas that need it the most as well as towards educating villagers on proper methods of hygiene and cleanliness in order to ensure the well will stay clean and safe.
