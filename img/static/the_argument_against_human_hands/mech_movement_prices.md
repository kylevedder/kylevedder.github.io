# Mechanical watch movement prices — sources and data points

Backing data for `movement_prices.png` and the napkin-math section of
*The Case Against Human Hands*. Each datum is listed with its source.
Prices are wholesale or small-quantity-replacement USD where stated;
ranges are kept as ranges (the plot uses midpoints).

U.S. City Average) series. Multipliers used (annual-average CPI; 2026

## Macro context (industry events shaded on the plot)

### Quartz crisis (1973–1983)

- Swiss watch production fell from a record 96M units (1974) to 45M (1983).
- Number of Swiss watchmakers fell from ~1,600 (1970) to ~600 (1983).
- Swiss workforce fell from ~90,000 to ~30,000.
- Mass-market mechanical movement prices "halved" as Hong Kong, Taiwan,
  China, Japan production scaled to fill the void.
- Sources:
  [Wikipedia — Quartz crisis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quartz_crisis),
  [Seiko Museum — The Quartz Crisis and Recovery of Swiss Watches](https://museum.seiko.co.jp/en/knowledge/relation_11/),
  [FEE — Markets in Time: The Rise, Fall, and Revival of Swiss Watchmaking](https://fee.org/articles/markets-in-time-the-rise-fall-and-revival-of-swiss-watchmaking/)

### Swatch / ETA supply restriction (2002–2018)

- 2002: Nicolas Hayek announces ETA will stop delivering ebauches to
  non-Swatch brands.
- 2012: Swiss Competition Commission orders phased reduction.
- Drove emergence and growth of Sellita SW200 as direct ETA 2824 substitute.
- Sources:
  [Wikipedia — ETA SA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ETA_SA),
  [aBlogToWatch — Swatch Group ETA monopoly probe](https://www.ablogtowatch.com/swatch-groups-eta-watch-movement-maker-oversteps-receives-unlawful-monopoly-probe-by-swiss-competition-commission/),
  [Professional Watches — ETA banned from selling to big brands in 2020](https://professionalwatches.com/editorial-eta-banned-from-selling-to-big-watch-brands-in-2020/)

## Pre-quartz-crisis: rough anchors

No clean public movement-only wholesale series has turned up for the
1950s-1970s. The best available web-indexed evidence is a mix of:

- direct movement or movement-list prices from old catalogs / Congressional
  testimony;
- complete-watch retail prices, with the movement cost inferred as a
  fraction of the complete watch;
- qualitative industry histories that explain why the series is not smooth
  (Roskopf/dollar-watch floor, Timex disposable construction, Japanese
  automation, then quartz destroying the low-end mechanical category).

For inferred movement prices below, the assumption depends on what the
source is pricing. Early jeweler listings that explicitly name the
movement get a high movement share (~40-60% of the complete-watch price).
Vertically integrated postwar retail watches get a lower factory movement
share (~15-30% of retail). Extremely cheap disposable watches get a high
share (~50-70%) because the movement is most of the product. These are
deliberately broad ranges: retail price includes retailer margin,
case/bracelet, dial/hands, distribution, and advertising.

Interpretation:

- The **cheap complete-watch floor** fell early: the Ingersoll/Roskopf
  lineage had already produced ~$20-30 real complete watches by the early
  1900s, but by using very crude movements.
- The **jeweled lever movement floor** appears to have fallen from hundreds
  of 2026 dollars in the 1900s to roughly tens/low-hundreds by the Timex
  and Japanese-automation era.
- By the late 1970s, the remaining price decline was not an open-ended
  mechanical learning curve. Quartz captured the cheap-timekeeping market,
  while mechanical movements survived either as disposable low-end
  constructions (Timex), Japanese commodity automatics (Miyota/Seiko), or
  Swiss/luxury supply-chain products.

The "easy wins were taken decades ago" framing is therefore still partly
inferential, but the added anchors make the shape clearer: a large decline
from early jeweled American movements to mid-century/1970s mass-market
mechanicals, followed by a much flatter floor for modern commodity
mechanicals.

## Notes on plot construction

- Y axis on `movement_prices.png` is **2026 USD**, CPI-adjusted from nominal
  year-of-quote prices.
- Lines between anchors are dotted to make clear that intermediate years
  are interpolated, not sourced.
- Launch-era anchors for the 8215 (1977), 2824-2 (1982), and SW200 (2008)
  are estimates — the only firm year-stamped wholesale anchor in the
  dataset is the 2009 Sinn quote of EUR 90 (≈ $126 nominal, ≈ $195 in
  2026 USD) for the 2824-2.

## Real-terms summary

| Movement | Launch (2026 USD) | 2024 (2026 USD) | Real change |
|---|---|---|---|
| Miyota 8215 | ~$162 (1977) | ~$21 mid | **−87%** |
| Seiko NH35 | ~$73 (2011) | ~$63 mid | −14% |
| ETA 2824-2 | ~$204 (1982) | ~$263 mid | **+29%** |
| Sellita SW200 | ~$171 (2008) | ~$204 mid | +19% |

The 8215 is the only series in the dataset that shows a textbook
learning-curve decline; even there, the decline is concentrated in the
first decade after launch and asymptotes well above zero. The Swiss
movements move *up* in real terms, which is monopoly behavior, not a
process-improvement curve.
